Personal Statement
(Joe) Zhe Ren
Assistant Professor · Department of Communications · California State University, Bakersfield

This statement accompanies my Working Personnel Action File in support of my application for tenure at California State University, Bakersfield (CSUB). I was appointed as an Assistant Professor in Fall 2023 with two years of credit towards tenure. Therefore, this is the fourth and final review of my probationary period, and I am seeking tenure and promotion. The essential criteria of the Department of Communications will be used to evaluate my performance, because this is not considered an early tenure/promotion request due to the two years of credit approved at the time of hire. Over the past three years, I have worked to develop my teaching, scholarly activity, and service in ways that reflect my commitment to this institution and to the field of digital media and design.

In teaching, I have developed and delivered a growing curriculum spanning seven course sections in AY 2025–2026 alone, including two newly designed courses — COMM 2038 (Visual Language) and COMM 3028 (Graphic Design from Prehistory to AI) — that have received strong student evaluations since their introduction. In scholarly activity, I have maintained an active research and creative practice across three years, participating in 12 film screenings, contributing as graphic designer to films that received 4 international festival awards, presenting at 3 international conferences, exhibiting work in 8 solo and group exhibitions, delivering 5 invited lectures and workshops, publishing in 3 publications, and receiving 5 grants in support of my research and creative work. In service, I have contributed actively at the departmental, college, university, community, and professional levels.

This statement provides a brief overview of my work across all three years at CSUB, with a focus on this year's accomplishments, and reflects my ongoing commitment to teaching excellence, original scholarship, and meaningful service to CSUB and the broader academic community.

Teaching
Teaching Philosophy

Creativity is one of the essential qualities of any artistic process. My teaching philosophy centers on helping students understand and develop the relationship between visual imagery and storytelling in the creative process. To inspire creativity requires an intricate interplay of deep sensibility, acute observation, unexpected imagination, unique expressiveness, and balanced control of the overall concept. I use visual juxtaposition and montage exercises to stimulate students' engagement with the creative process.

A thoughtful integration of aesthetics, functionality, and technology forms the foundation of effective media art and design education. From introductory courses to advanced studies in digital media, I help students understand that whether it be traditional illustration, digital design, or emerging technologies — these are only tools for creative expression. Media art is founded on a visual language that helps define both our culture and notions of audience for media work, preparing students for their future careers. In my classes, students are encouraged to move beyond technique by using their technical skills to support conceptual understanding. A key focus in the early stages of instruction is "learning to see" — cultivating visual awareness, developing critical observation, and exploring how form and meaning are interconnected.

I believe that media art and design are deeply connected to other academic disciplines. Students should be encouraged to be adaptable, capable of acquiring new skills, and open to interdisciplinary approaches. Collaborative learning between educators and students fosters a sense of community and allows us to explore the intersections of contemporary design, digital media, science, culture, and the broader social environment.

Over three years, my teaching portfolio has grown steadily in both breadth and range, expanding across in-person and online delivery formats and incorporating newly designed courses each year.

I began in Fall 2023 with foundational courses in Media Arts (COMM 2310), Design for Print & Online Media (COMM 3170), and 3D Animation (COMM 3350). COMM 3170 continued into Spring 2024 alongside Media Arts and the addition of Digital Video Production (COMM 3320), further developing my engagement with production-centered coursework. In Fall 2024, I took on two concurrent online sections of Video Game Analysis (COMM 3038) while continuing to teach Media Arts in person. Drawing on my prior teaching experience, I structured these asynchronous sections with clear feedback cadences, consistent pacing, and accessible support — an approach that continued to evolve and strengthen across subsequent online sections. Video Game Analysis continued into Spring 2025 alongside the return of Digital Video Production.

In AY 2025–2026, I also designed and developed two new courses: COMM 2038 (Visual Language) and COMM 3028 (Graphic Design from Prehistory to AI). Visual Language provides a foundation-level entry point for students developing visual literacy, and Graphic Design from Prehistory to AI offers a historically and conceptually grounded study of design in the context of emerging technologies. Student evaluations for both courses were encouraging: COMM 2038 scored 4.77 out of 5.00 in Fall 2025, and COMM 3028 received 4.62 in Fall 2025 and 4.77 in Spring 2026.

I joined CSUB with 2 years credits toward tenure. During my first two years, I carried a one-course release as part of my new faculty appointment, and in Spring 2026 I received an additional one-course release through the RSCA/RCU Initiative Award for my research project A Journey to Kern River and Beyond – Media Arts Project. This protected time supported both my research and course development during the early years of my appointment.

This trajectory reflects a growing teaching load alongside continued investment in course development and curriculum innovation.

Across all three years, my approach to teaching has remained grounded in a consistent set of principles while continuing to evolve. I draw on design history, professional practice, and my membership in the Association of Chinese Artists in American Academia to shape projects that connect students to the broader context of their field. In every course, I emphasize learning through doing — through demonstrations, self-critique, small-group feedback, and individualized support — while encouraging students to move beyond technique toward conceptual thinking. What has developed over time is a clearer sense of how to calibrate these approaches across different course formats: the balance between demonstration and critique looks different in an in-person studio than in a fully asynchronous online section, and I continue to refine that calibration each semester based on what students tell me is working.

Peer Evaluations

I invited a senior colleague to conduct a formal peer observation of my COMM 3028 class this year. The observation documented a well-organized, engaging classroom environment with clear learning objectives and meaningful student participation. I value peer evaluation as an opportunity for ongoing reflection and improvement, and I plan to continue this practice in future semesters.

A classroom observation of COMM 3038 Video Game Analysis (Fall 2024) noted a perceived lack of assessments. The course includes three types of assessment — weekly reflection posts and discussion board participation, activity assignment, and a final analytical project and presentation — organized within the weekly modules throughout the semester. Following this observation, I restructured the weekly modules to make the assessment expectations more clearly visible at each stage of the course, and added a quiz component to further strengthen the assessment structure and provide students with more frequent and varied opportunities to demonstrate their learning.

Future Research Plans

I am currently co-investigating a LIFT (Learning Innovation for Future-Forward Teaching) grant proposal with Professor Weimin Zhang at San Francisco State University, titled AI-Integrated Cinematic Storytelling: Multi-Campus Collaborative Production and Future-Forward Creative Workflows.
